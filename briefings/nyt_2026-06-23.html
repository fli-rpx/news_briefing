Cluster 1: The Iran Opening

The Deal That May Not Be a Deal

The Trump administration temporarily lifted oil sanctions against Iran on Monday, calling it a result of "ongoing productive" talks at the

Bürgenstock Resort in Switzerland. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent issued a 60-day license authorizing Iranian oil purchases in

U.S. dollars — a sharp reversal of the "maximum pressure" policy Trump championed since withdrawing from the JCPOA in 2018.

Vice President JD Vance, leading the U.S. negotiating team, said Tehran agreed to allow IAEA inspectors to visit nuclear sites. "A

major milestone for the American people," he called it. But Iran's foreign ministry spokesman, Esmail Baghaei, told state news agency

IRNA that Tehran had made "no new commitments" on nuclear inspections. The IAEA did not immediately respond to Vance's claim.

The gap between U.S. declarations and Iranian denials is the story's central tension. Two communication mechanisms were created

— one for the Strait of Hormuz, one for the Lebanon cease-fire. Iran's negotiators traveled to Oman to discuss "fees" for Hormuz

whose truce is linked to the broader negotiations.

Cluster 2: Global Economic Turbulence

Markets in Freefall

Global stock markets shuddered on Tuesday as AI enthusiasm appeared to reach its limits. South Korea's Kospi plunged 10.5%,

triggering a circuit breaker. The Nasdaq fell 2.2% at the open. SpaceX lost $600 billion in market value over three trading sessions. "If

today's price action points to A.I. exhaustion," Geoffrey Yu of BNY noted, "fears will grow concerning the global economy's ability to

generate a clear and diversified growth narrative."

China's Rare-Earth Grip Tightens

China banned rare-earth metal shipments to two U.S. manufacturers at the center of the Trump administration's effort to rebuild

domestic supply chains. The move risks reigniting trade tensions. China controls roughly 90% of global light rare-earth supply. The

G7 pledged at last week's summit to reduce dependence, calling for no more than 60% from one country by 2030 — but the pledge

has no enforcement mechanism, interim targets, or budget.

US Oil Passes on Market Share

U.S. oil production is forecast to top 14 million barrels a day, but the industry is not capitalizing on the disruption to Persian Gulf

producers. "I am skeptical that the U.S. really has the means or the wherewithal to actually gain share," said Barclays analyst J. David

Anderson. Shareholders want steady profits, not boom-to-bust cycles. Last year's low prices led companies to shed employees and

equipment.

Brexit's Decade-Long Toll

Ten years after the vote, Britain's economy is 4% to 6% smaller than if it had stayed in the EU. Trade and investment are lower.

Nearly half of Britons say Brexit went worse than expected. Just over half would support rejoining. The country is about to get its

seventh prime minister since the vote.

Who is affected: Korean tech investors watching a 10.5% single-day loss. U.S. manufacturers dependent on Chinese rare earths.

American oil company shareholders. British citizens facing a smaller economy. The next UK prime minister inheriting the cost.

neither water nor tea, focused only on leaving.

Ukraine Escalates Crimea Attacks

Ukraine is stepping up an air campaign to isolate Crimea. Summer camps are canceled. Gas is nowhere to be found. Electricity is

flickering. Ukraine's defense minister, Mykhailo Fedorov, gloated on Facebook: "We are closing the beach season in Crimea."

Ukrainian forces struck oil depots, gas compressor stations, air defense systems, and radar systems. The goal: turn Crimea into an

Cuba's Schools Collapse

Cuba's fuel crisis, caused by the U.S. oil blockade, has brought schools to a standstill. Sergio Alfonso Vásquez, 33, a farmer, takes

his children on horseback to school. "My children rarely go to school. They go, but the teachers don't come," he said. "I'm afraid

because they aren't learning anything." The government cut schools to half-days in February, ended the school year two weeks early,

and scrapped college entrance exams.

De La Espriella gives Trump a key ally. He has ruled out a "US-led incursion" but supports intelligence sharing — a line that

historically blurs.
