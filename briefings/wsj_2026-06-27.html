The Strait of Hormuz Crisis Enters Day Three

Fighting around the strategic Strait of Hormuz extended into a third consecutive day on Saturday, with Bahrain coming under attack from Iranian drones and a commercial tanker struck while transiting the 21-mile waterway. The widening confrontation threatens a fragile ceasefire deal brokered earlier this year between Washington and Tehran.

U.S. launches fresh strikes. The United States responded with fresh military strikes against Iranian positions after the cargo-ship attack was deemed a violation of the existing ceasefire. President Trump characterized the Iranian action as a breach of the agreement's terms. Tehran retaliated with its own attacks across the region, ratcheting up fears of a broader Middle Eastern war. The tit-for-tat cycle — strike, counterstrike, diplomatic overture, renewed hostilities — has become the defining rhythm of the conflict since its escalation in February.

Naval base devastation forced a U.S. recalculation. A WSJ investigation published this week examined how Iran devastated an American naval facility in the region, marking one of the most significant direct strikes on U.S. forces in the conflict. The attack forced American military planners to reassess force posture in the Gulf, revealing vulnerabilities in base defense against Iranian asymmetric tactics — particularly drone swarms and precision missiles. The incident has influenced thinking across the Pentagon about the sustainability of a large-scale American naval presence in waters within Iranian striking distance.

Iran tests the deal. Iran's strike on a cargo vessel in the Strait directly challenges the Trump administration's negotiated agreement to reopen the strategic waterway to commercial shipping. The attack raises urgent questions about the durability of the security guarantees underpinning the deal. Shipping traffic through Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of the world's oil passes — remains the single most critical variable for global energy markets. Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari's warning about potential rate hikes this year, discussed in Cluster 2, is informed in part by the risk that Hormuz disruptions reignite energy-driven inflation.

Probability estimate: 35% probability that the current ceasefire framework collapses entirely within the next 30 days, triggering open-ended military escalation in the Gulf. Basis: Iran's demonstrated willingness to test the deal's boundaries (cargo-ship attack), the absence of a credible enforcement mechanism, and the deteriorating trust between negotiating parties documented in WSJ's reporting on U.S.-Iranian "trash talk" disrupting peace negotiations.

Probability estimate: 20% probability that mediators secure a comprehensive framework covering both Lebanon and Hormuz within 90 days. Basis: International mediators have put forward a plan attempting to reduce tensions simultaneously in Lebanon and the Strait — a level of diplomatic ambition that, if successful, would represent a breakthrough. The odds are low given the current trajectory.

Diplomatic Paradox: Dollar-Denominated Oil Sales

In a policy shift with decades-deep significance, the U.S. has permitted Iran to sell oil and receive payment in U.S. dollars for the first time since sanctions architecture was erected. The move is a calculated concession — designed to create economic entanglement and a stake in stability for Tehran. Iranian officials estimate the reopening of the Strait alongside Gulf states could generate a $40 billion economic windfall. The figure underscores the enormous economic stakes: Iran's willingness to re-enter normalized commerce is both a bargaining chip and a strategic vulnerability, as the U.S. can potentially make dollar-denominated access revocable — a form of structural leverage.

Side Development: Iranians on Turkish Dance Floor

A lighter WSJ feature captures the human dimension of the crisis: Iranian civilians vacationing on Turkey's Mediterranean coast, seeking escape from the tensions at home. The piece offers an understated portrait of life under the shadow of potential war — ordinary Iranians going about their lives even as the Hormuz crisis escalates and diplomatic channels remain uncertain.

Mediators Offer Comprehensive Plan

International mediators have put forward a plan attempting to reduce tensions simultaneously in Lebanon and the Strait of Hormuz — a level of diplomatic ambition that, if successful, would represent a breakthrough. The proposal seeks to link progress on the Lebanon front with Hormuz de-escalation, creating a framework that addresses both theaters of Iran-linked conflict. The plan faces steep odds given the current trajectory of escalation, but represents the most structured diplomatic initiative since the crisis escalated in February.

Trump's Shifting Rhetoric Under the Microscope

WSJ's linguistic analysis of President Trump's evolving public statements on the Iran conflict reveals significant shifts in tone and policy positioning over the course of the crisis. The analysis tracks movement from hawkish threats in the early weeks to more conciliatory language as the political and military landscape evolved. The rhetorical shifts appear to reflect both strategic calculation and domestic political pressures — the sort of inconsistency that, per Sun Tzu's framework, creates planning friction for both allies and adversaries. The rhetorical volatility has become a factor in the peace negotiations themselves: escalating personal insults between U.S. and Iranian officials — what WSJ characterizes as "trash talk" — is actively undermining already fragile diplomatic back-channels.

Israel's Lebanon Dilemma

The Israeli military finds itself caught in what WSJ describes as "a political no man's land in Lebanon with no clear exit strategy." Domestic political divisions in both Israel and Lebanon are complicating military operations, with the IDF unable to execute a decisive campaign or withdraw without strategic loss. The situation represents what might be called strategic immobilization at the institutional level — stuck, immobilized, neither able to fight effectively nor to disengage.

Ukraine's Crimea Campaign

Sustained Ukrainian strikes on military and infrastructure targets in Crimea are transforming what was once a prized Russian acquisition into a strategic liability for President Vladimir Putin. The attacks have disrupted Russian military logistics and supply lines in the occupied peninsula. The quantitative accumulation of Ukrainian drone capability appears to have crossed a qualitative threshold, with Russian air-defense systems transitioning from "functioning but stressed" to "overwhelmed and penetrated." The WSJ opinion section separately argues that "The Russian Economy Looks More Vulnerable Each Day," citing accumulated sanctions pressure and war expenditures eroding fiscal stability — a factor that constrains Moscow's ability to reinforce its Crimea position.

Side Development: Italy's Meloni Model

Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni has managed to build the most stable Italian government in years despite a combative political style. WSJ's profile examines how her coalition management and relationship with the Trump administration have contributed to her political durability. Italy's relative stability stands in contrast to political turbulence elsewhere in Europe and offers a case study in disciplined governance — the unglamorous work of maintaining relationships and systems day by day.

Defense-Industrial: Army Leases Land for Critical Minerals

The U.S. Army will lease land on military bases for the production of critical minerals needed for defense and technology supply chains. The initiative is part of broader efforts to reduce dependence on foreign sources of strategic materials — including rare-earth elements currently dominated by Chinese supply chains. A WSJ opinion piece, "A Senate Advance on Cheaper Weapons," praises parallel Senate efforts to reform defense procurement processes to develop cheaper, more effective weapons systems.

Saudi Restarts Crude Loadings — Market Signal

Saudi Arabia has restarted crude oil loadings, signaling a normalization of supply flows despite the ongoing Hormuz tensions. The restart contributed to WTI retreating toward prewar levels (~$69.49/bbl, estimated), though the ceasefire's fragility means a renewed supply disruption remains a material risk. The Fed's Divergent Signals

The Federal Reserve is sending unusually mixed signals about the path of interest rates, creating uncertainty for markets already navigating the intersection of Hormuz-driven energy risk and AI-driven inflation pressures.

Kashkari opens the door to hikes. Minneapolis Federal Reserve President Neel Kashkari stated that the central bank "may have to raise interest rates again this year if inflation does not continue to moderate." His comments — among the more hawkish from any FOMC participant this cycle — suggest that the fight against inflation is not yet over and that the data-center-driven demand pressures could force additional tightening.

Williams signals a pause. New York Federal Reserve President John Williams offered a contrasting assessment, stating that the "current monetary policy stance is well positioned to bring inflation back to the Fed's 2% target." Markets interpreted the remarks as signaling a potential pause in rate adjustments.

The Atlanta Fed leadership vacuum. The search for a new president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta has stalled amid disagreements among board members over candidates and the direction of monetary policy. The delay highlights internal tensions within the Fed system and means one of the twelve regional bank presidents — each of whom votes on the FOMC on a rotating basis — will remain unfilled.

Warsh builds a team. Kevin Warsh, a former Federal Reserve governor mentioned as a potential future Fed chair, has hired two veteran Fed economists as advisers. The move signals that Warsh is building a policy team and positioning himself for a potential return to central banking leadership. The appointments have drawn attention in financial circles, particularly given the uncertainty about Chair Jerome Powell's future.

The Data-Center Inflation Wave

The massive boom in data-center construction — driven by demand for AI computing power — is contributing to what some economists are calling a third wave of inflation. The construction and energy demands of data centers are pushing up costs in related sectors: commercial real estate, industrial electrical equipment, and power generation. The phenomenon presents a structural challenge for the Fed: the same technological force that was deflationary in 2023 (AI automation reducing labor costs) has become inflationary in 2026 (infrastructure capex pushing up demand). As noted in Cluster 3, Apple and Microsoft have both cited rising component costs in announcing price increases on consumer hardware.

Probability estimate: 40% probability that the Fed raises rates at least once before year-end 2026. Basis: Kashkari's explicit signal, the data-center inflation wave adding structural pressure, and the Hormuz risk premium potentially re-emerging if the ceasefire collapses. Countervailing: Williams's dovish signal and moderating consumer sentiment data.

Consumer Sentiment and the Gasoline Dividend

U.S. consumer sentiment improved in June as gasoline prices moderated from earlier highs. The improvement provides some relief to household budgets that had been squeezed by energy costs since the Iran conflict began in February. Consumer spending — the primary engine of U.S. GDP — may receive a modest boost, though persistent inflation concerns and elevated interest rates continue to restrain discretionary spending. The improvement is consistent with the retreat of oil prices to prewar levels (~$69.49/bbl, estimated), though as the Geopolitics cluster documents, the Hormuz ceasefire remains fragile and a renewed spike in energy prices remains a tail risk.

Global Central Banking Roundup

Eurozone: Household inflation expectations cooled in May, a welcome sign for the European Central Bank as it gathers for its annual forum in Sintra, Portugal. Central bankers from around the world are attending the forum, which market participants are closely monitoring for shifts in policy guidance.

Japan: A hawkish member of the Bank of Japan's policy board indicated there is "scope to bring the policy rate closer to 2%," signaling a potential continuation of the BOJ's tightening cycle. Japan's gradual shift away from its decades-long ultra-loose monetary policy continues, with implications for global carry trades.

Mexico: The Bank of Mexico held its benchmark interest rate steady, pausing after a series of adjustments as inflation moderates while remaining above target.

Australia: Strong employment data is keeping the possibility of further rate increases on the table for the Reserve Bank of Australia. The tight labor market is putting upward pressure on wages and prices.

Trade Policy: Trump's 100% Tariff Threat

President Trump has threatened to impose 100% tariffs on European countries that levy digital services taxes on American technology companies — including the EU's Digital Markets Act (DMA) designation of Amazon and Microsoft's cloud services, discussed in Cluster 3. The threat escalates ongoing trade tensions at a moment when the U.S. needs European cooperation on Iran containment and Gulf security. European leaders have pushed back, warning of retaliatory measures. A WSJ opinion piece, "The Low-Tax Case for Tariffs," argues that tariffs can raise government revenue without increasing income or corporate tax rates, contributing to the intra-conservative debate over trade policy.

A WSJ opinion piece criticizes Illinois Governor J.B. Pritzker for imposing a series of new taxes characterized as "junk taxes," arguing the increases are hurting Illinois's economy and driving residents to other states.

AI Regulation: Whiplash in Washington and Brussels

The AI regulatory landscape is sending contradictory signals that are making strategic planning difficult for the industry's leading players.

OpenAI restricts access. OpenAI has limited access to its latest AI models, citing national security concerns raised by government agencies. The decision reflects growing tension between AI development velocity and government oversight — a recurring pattern of "innovation → fear → restriction → adaptation."

Anthropic ban partially rolled back. In a move that underscores the whiplash, the Trump administration has partially rolled back restrictions on Anthropic's AI models that were previously banned over security concerns. The decision represents a shift in approach — allowing certain Anthropic capabilities to be deployed while maintaining other restrictions.

EU targets cloud. The European Union has indicated that Amazon Web Services and Microsoft Azure should be subject to the Digital Markets Act, the bloc's landmark competition law. The designation would impose stricter rules on how these companies operate in Europe — a move that could reshape the competitive landscape of cloud computing. The DMA designation provides the predicate for Trump's 100% tariff threat against European digital-services taxes, creating a circular diplomatic-economic tension between Washington and Brussels.

Chip Wars and Computing Infrastructure

BYD's autonomous-driving chip. Chinese electric-vehicle maker BYD has unveiled a new in-house chip designed for autonomous driving technology. The move represents a significant step in BYD's vertical integration strategy and reduces reliance on external semiconductor suppliers — particularly relevant given ongoing U.S.-China technology-export controls.

DeepSeek's expansion. Chinese AI startup DeepSeek plans to double its workforce after securing a new round of significant funding. The company's rapid growth reflects the intense global competition in the AI sector and challenges American assumptions about maintaining technological primacy.

Data-center inflation (cross-reference Cluster 2). The data-center boom driving a third wave of inflation is itself a business-strategy story: the buildout of AI computing infrastructure is creating supply-chain bottlenecks, driving up memory and component costs that are being passed on to consumers, as Apple and Microsoft have both demonstrated this week.

Consumer Hardware Price Hikes

Apple raised prices on its Mac and iPad lines by $200 or more on select models, citing rising component and manufacturing costs — the same data-center-driven inflation documented in Cluster 2 cascading through the supply chain to consumers.

Microsoft increased prices on Xbox Series X and Series S consoles, citing soaring memory and component costs. The move follows similar price adjustments by competitors in the gaming hardware market.

Amazon's India Bet

Amazon has announced plans to invest an additional $13 billion in India by 2030, expanding its cloud infrastructure and e-commerce operations. The investment underscores India's importance as a growth market for global tech companies and comes amid evolving regulations for foreign technology firms in New Delhi.

Anthropic veterans' new venture. Former employees of AI safety company Anthropic have launched a startup aimed at helping scientists develop their own AI models — democratizing AI development by providing tools and platforms for researchers outside the major labs.

Scaled Cognition's reliability thesis. AI startup Scaled Cognition has proposed a new methodology for developing more reliable and predictable AI systems, focusing on interpretability and safety rather than raw performance metrics.

The Democratic Civil War Intensifies

The Democratic Party is in the throes of an internal conflict that WSJ describes as a "civil war," with the far-left faction gaining significant ground against the moderate establishment. The ideological struggle is playing out across multiple fronts simultaneously.

The California billionaire tax. California's new tax targeting billionaires has ignited a fierce internal battle within the party, pitting progressive advocates against moderate and business-friendly factions. The policy has prompted some wealthy individuals to consider leaving the state — an accelerating outmigration trend — and reflects broader national divisions over wealth taxation.

Mamdani's NYC sweep. A sweep by progressive candidates aligned with Zohran Mamdani in New York City elections is deepening strains between Jewish voters and the Democratic Party over Israel policy and antisemitism concerns. The results signal the growing power of the party's left wing in major urban centers, with implications for the 2026 midterms and beyond.

Wall Street's reckoning. A WSJ headline captures the strategic dilemma for established power: "Wall Street Realizes It Needs More Than Money to Counter Mamdani." Financial interests have discovered that grassroots organizing and moral messaging are proving more effective than traditional campaign spending — a reversal of the usual assumption that money dominates politics.

Socialism on the march. WSJ examines the rising influence of socialist and progressive economic policies in both New York and Britain, analyzing how left-wing ideas are gaining traction in two major financial centers. The trend has implications for tax policy, regulation, and business investment decisions.

The Policy Landscape

Obamacare subsidy withdrawal. Millions of Americans have canceled their Affordable Care Act health insurance plans following the withdrawal of federal subsidies. The policy change has led to significant coverage losses, with the impact concentrated in lower-income communities and swing states.

Budget spigots wide open. Federal spending is surging as Congress approves new budget outlays driven by foreign policy and defense commitments. Despite deficit concerns, both parties have found common ground in increasing expenditures — spending driven by crisis momentum rather than strategic priority setting.

Immigration enforcement shifts. The Florida detention center nicknamed "Alligator Alcatraz" is closing after years of controversy and legal challenges. The facility was known for its remote location and harsh conditions; its closure represents a shift in immigration enforcement posture.

DOJ antitrust stance. A senior Department of Justice official has privately told staff that he prefers to avoid antitrust trials, signaling a potential shift in enforcement approach that could affect ongoing and future cases against major technology companies — a development with direct implications for the tech-sector stories in Cluster 3.

宋鸿兵观察：2026年6月27日——美元潮汐的第三次回流

再看美联储的分裂信号。卡什卡里暗示年内可能加息，威廉姆斯则主张按兵不动。这种分歧掩盖了一个深层事实：AI数据中心的基建狂潮正在制造第三波通胀——这不是暂时性价格上涨，而是资本支出驱动的结构性价格压力。人工智能泡沫从2023年的通缩力量（自动化降成本）反转成了2026年的通胀引擎（基础设施挤占资源）。这正是我多年反复强调的"债务-泡沫-通胀"传导链条：廉价货币催生资产泡沫，泡沫需要实体投资来兑现，投资推高要素成本，成本最终转化为通胀，通胀迫使央行收紧，收紧刺破泡沫。
