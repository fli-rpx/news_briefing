The Strait of Hormuz Escalation: Conflict Without a Budget

The U.S. military campaign near the Strait of Hormuz continues to intensify. The administration has reimposed a blockade of Iran, conducted strikes on Iranian bridge infrastructure, deployed sea drones, and signaled interest in expanding military operations. Oil prices have surged 10% in a single week, with crude up 3.57% on the day alone.

The operational backbone is cracking. The GOP war-funding package is smaller than President Trump has been seeking, and passage is uncertain due to party divides. The "GOP's Budget Reconciliation Bust" reveals that even under unified government, the party cannot agree on a coherent fiscal agenda.

A self-reinforcing doom loop is forming: oil spike вҶ’ diesel inflation вҶ’ CPI pressure вҶ’ higher bond yields вҶ’ higher debt service costs вҶ’ larger deficits вҶ’ more issuance. Every week of stalemate bleeds U.S. fiscal capacity without forcing Iranian concessions.

Calibrated view: 70% probability that the U.S. avoids a full-scale ground engagement in Iran within 90 days. The funding gap (60% probability the GOP fails to pass the current war package as-is) is the primary constraint вҖ” not Iranian military capability.

Iran's Economy Under Pressure: Does Attrition Work?

The U.S. is betting that "renewed pressure will convince Tehran to release its hold on the Strait of Hormuz." The squeeze on Iran's battered economy is tightening, with the administration targeting supply routes and economic lifelines.

Iran is fighting a war of attrition on favorable terrain вҖ” narrow seas, asymmetric options (mines, drones, anti-ship missiles, proxies). The U.S. is fighting a war of escalation on unfavorable fiscal ground. "The clock favors Tehran" вҖ” not because Iran is stronger, but because the U.S. fiscal position erodes with every day the conflict continues.

Chevron's announcement that it will explore a pipeline bypassing the Strait of Hormuz for Iraqi oil is the most telling signal. When private capital starts routing around a chokepoint, it is betting that the chokepoint is structurally compromised, not temporarily disrupted.

Calibrated view: 60% probability that Iran does not release its hold on the Strait within 6 months. Chevron's pipeline exploration (35% probability of reaching FID within 18 months) is a structural hedge.

Oil, Diesel, and the Inflation Transmission Belt

Crude oil is headed for a 10% weekly gain, driven by Iran escalation. But the more structurally significant story is the "Sharp Drop in Diesel Supplies Threatens to Rev Up Inflation Again." U.S. distillate inventories stand at 104 million barrels вҖ” near the 5-year low, 12% below the seasonal average.

  • Diesel is a direct input to trucking (70% of U.S. freight) and agriculture
  • Refining capacity is structurally constrained, not cyclically tight
  • Transport cost shocks transmit to CPI with a 6вҖ“12 week lag
  • The last time diesel inventories hit this level (March 2022, 102 million barrels), headline CPI was running at 8.5%

The compound risk: higher oil prices вҶ’ higher diesel вҶ’ higher inflation вҶ’ higher bond yields вҶ’ higher interest costs on federal debt вҶ’ larger deficits вҶ’ larger bond issuance. A doom loop with no political circuit breaker.

Calibrated view: 75% probability that diesel prices feed into a higher-than-expected September CPI print.

Greg Ip's Bond Market Warning: The 24th Time or the One That Matters?

Greg Ip's Capital Account column delivers a characteristically careful warning: Treasury is "issuing record debt into a market increasingly unfriendly to profligate governments." Foreign official holdings of Treasuries peaked in 2021 and have declined modestly. The marginal buyer today is domestic institutional money. If foreign central banks reduce rollover rates, the yield response could be swift and non-linear.

"Bond vigilantes have not yet arrived, but the structural conditions are in place." China's reduced Treasury holdings are a slow-motion decoupling of the deepest financial interdependence in history.

Calibrated view: 45% probability of a 10-year Treasury yield spike above 5.5% within 6 months. The diesel-inflation channel is the most likely catalyst.
