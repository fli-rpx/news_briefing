The Iran Deal — A Fragile Ceasefire

THE IRAN DEAL — A FRAGILE CEASEFIRE HAUNTED BY OBAMA'S SHADOW

Articles: 1 (Trump defends deal), 2 (text of agreement), 3 (Obama comparison), 7 (G7 summit), 16 (oil reserves), 20 (fertilizer prices)

### The Deal on Paper

A senior U.S. official disclosed the full text of the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding on Wednesday, reading it aloud on a conference call with reporters. The agreement declares an "immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon." It outlines a $300 billion plan for Iran's reconstruction, lifts restrictions on Iranian oil exports, and commits both sides to begin nuclear negotiations within a 60-day window. Crucially, the Lebanon clause — requiring respect for Lebanon's territorial integrity — amounts to a "remarkable rejection by the United States of Israel's concerns about the threat posed by Hezbollah," according to NYT annotations. Israel has signaled it does not feel bound by Lebanon-related provisions.

### Trump's Expletive-Laden Defense

At the G7 summit in Évian-les-Bains, Trump lashed out at critics comparing his deal unfavorably to Barack Obama's 2015 nuclear accord. "They laughed at Obama, and they said he's a stupid son of a bitch," Trump told reporters, escalating a rhetorical war with his predecessor. He threatened to "go right back to dropping bombs right smack in the middle of their head" if Iran violates the terms.

The comparison is the deal's central vulnerability. David Sanger's analysis frames Trump as "caught in what could best be described as the Obama-deal bind": the 2015 JCPOA was a comprehensive nuclear framework; Trump's agreement is a cease-fire with deferred nuclear talks. They are "completely different in nature," but Trump cannot escape the comparison because he campaigned against the JCPOA, killed it, and started a war to prove its inadequacy. Now he has less to show for it.

### G7 Whiplash

The summit followed a script of studied harmony — European leaders praised the Iran deal as a "breakthrough" and signed a unified statement on Ukraine — until Trump's final-day disruption. He warned he might rip up the deal, questioned U.S. commitment to Ukraine, and whispered "Greenland" at the European Council president. Macron, who managed Trump through a private dinner at Versailles, defended his approach: "When it came to the security of Denmark and the Greenland issue, when it came to the territorial integrity of Ukraine and to peace and security in Europe, I was never ambiguous or weak."

### The Economic Underbelly

Two articles trace the war's economic damage to the heartland. The world is "quickly depleting its stores of oil," with U.S. government stockpiles poised to hit their lowest levels since 1983. "Soon enough, we'll run out of shock absorbers," warned Antoine Halff of Kayrros. Meanwhile, Mosaic — one of the world's largest fertilizer companies — is losing money because sulfur is stuck in the Strait of Hormuz. Ben Pratt, Mosaic's vice president, told the Times: "If we're losing money every ton, the total losses can mount quickly." Farmers are cutting back. Recovery of normal fertilizer pricing "looks more like a 2028 story than a 2027 one," said agricultural economist Shawn Arita.

### The Habeas Corpus Debate

Last spring, Will Scharf — an arch-conservative lawyer serving as White House staff secretary — wrote a secret memo to the chief of staff warning against suspending habeas corpus rights for undocumented immigrants. Stamped "confidential" and dated April 29, 2025, the memo was "a blinking red warning light" that the administration was debating what Scharf's legal analysis concluded was unconstitutional: the suspension of a right enshrined in Article I, suspended only in war or invasion. The revelation that the White House actively weighed this step — encouraged by Trump, pushed by Stephen Miller — documents a constitutional near-miss from the early months of Trump's second term.

### The Intelligence Nomination Hostage

Trump pulled Jay Clayton's nomination for director of national intelligence hours before his confirmation hearing, all but guaranteeing that Bill Pulte — a loyalist with no national security experience — will take the job as acting DNI. The move was explicitly transactional: Trump wanted to pressure Congress to pass a strict voter ID law by holding FISA reauthorization hostage. Senator John Thune warned the gambit would "tank" the surveillance bill. The Intelligence Committee's bipartisan plan to confirm Clayton quickly and renew FISA collapsed.

### Anthropic Under the Microscope

On Friday, executives at AI company Anthropic received less than 90 minutes' notice that the White House wanted them to take down their newest models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, over national security concerns. Six days later, the company's roughly 3,000 employees still have no clear answers. The administration changed its rationale multiple times — first citing foreign access, then a major vulnerability. "Does anyone know what to believe?" one employee asked in an internal chat. CEO Dario Amodei met with administration officials but secured no breakthrough.

### FEMA's Hollowing

Cameron Hamilton, Trump's nominee to lead FEMA, acknowledged at his confirmation hearing that 20% staff cuts "may challenge the nation's readiness for disasters" but expressed "full faith" in the workforce. Hamilton was fired last year from the acting role after contradicting Trump's assertion that FEMA should be abolished. The administration now appears to be retreating from elimination plans but has not restored staffing.

### Dismantling Wind Energy

The Interior Department paid Invenergy $65 million to cancel four offshore wind leases — the third such deal, bringing total government spending on wind project cancellations to roughly $1.5 billion. Invenergy will redirect the money toward natural gas and geothermal projects.

### Haiti Protections and Due Process

Newly released internal emails show the Homeland Security Department terminated deportation protections for more than 350,000 Haitians last June without obtaining required input from the State Department. The revelation could complicate a Supreme Court case expected by end of June. The ruling may affect about 1 million migrants from more than a dozen countries.

### DOJ Sues New York

The Justice Department sued the New York State Health Department over an $11 billion home health care program, accusing officials of failing to police a vendor that siphoned off millions. The suit names Amir Bassiri and Dr. James McDonald but not Governor Kathy Hochul. A Hochul spokeswoman called it "another sad attempt to weaponize the justice system." The article notes the DOJ has "increasingly pursued President Trump's political opponents."

### Georgia's Redistricting Victory

Georgia Republican leaders shelved plans to redraw congressional maps — designed to erase U.S. House seats held by Black Democrats — less than an hour before a special session was to begin. The retreat came after civil rights activists and protesters assembled at the state capitol in Atlanta, "the cradle of the civil rights movement." Speaker Jon Burns announced the decision as the crowd "erupted in cheers." The protesters had not fought — they had occupied symbolic ground, and the opposition folded.

### Ossoff: The Reluctant Star

Jon Ossoff, 39, is the most vulnerable Democratic senator seeking re-election, but a lot has gone right. He has proved a formidable fundraiser. His fiery anti-Trump rhetoric draws millions online. Governor Kemp passed on running against him. And on Tuesday, Georgia Republicans nominated Representative Mike Collins, a Trump loyalist, to face him — a candidate Ossoff helped engineer by quietly undermining the more moderate Derek Dooley in the primary.

Ossoff refuses to discuss presidential ambitions, but the speculation grows. "He is not trying to be a national star," said Jason Carter. "He's trying to be a good senator."

### Trump's Georgia Curse Continues

Trump's pick for Georgia governor, Lt. Gov. Burt Jones, lost the Republican primary runoff to Rick Jackson, a health care executive. It was the second time this month a Trump-endorsed gubernatorial candidate lost (following Randy Feenstra in Iowa). An election denier also lost the secretary of state runoff. The pattern: Georgia — which rejected Trump's 2020 lies, rejected his 2021 Senate picks, and now rejects his gubernatorial pick — continues to vex him.

### Clinton's Postmortem

Hillary Clinton told The New Yorker's David Remnick that Biden's decision to run for re-election was a "terrible mistake for himself, his legacy and for the country." If Biden had passed the torch, Clinton argued, a competitive primary would have produced a winner "who would have beaten Donald Trump." The DNC's autopsy of the 2024 defeat was described as "incomplete, error-filled."

### Crypto's Alabama Bet

The crypto industry's super PAC, Fairshake, has spent $9.8 million on ads in Alabama to elect Representative Barry Moore, a pro-crypto Republican. Total spending in the race: $12.1 million. Moore faces a competitive primary runoff against Jared Hudson, a retired Navy SEAL.

### A Jewish Community Fractures

In New York's 10th District — one of the most progressive and most Jewish districts in America — Representative Dan Goldman is fighting for his political life against Brad Lander. The issue: Israel and Gaza. Goldman marched in the Israel Day parade with Israeli officials; Lander canvassed with a group that has backed genocide claims against Israel. "It's not just between Lander and Goldman — it's between us and ourselves," said Gili Getz, an Israeli American filmmaker. Lander appears to hold a significant lead, though polling on New York primaries is scant.

Rick Jackson — the health care executive who beat Trump's endorsement. Hillary Clinton — still processing 2016 through the lens of 2024. ### The B-52 Crash

On Monday, a B-52 bomber crashed shortly after takeoff from Edwards Air Force Base, killing all eight crew members. The aircraft burst into flames visible across the Mojave Desert. The cause is under investigation; officials said it could take six months to determine. The test flight was part of an Air Force initiative to upgrade the plane's outdated radar and avionics — systems "long vulnerable to antiaircraft systems" on a platform first introduced in 1955.

Jeromy Smith, 32, a flight test engineer and father of two, had just returned from paternity leave. "All the time, he would talk about" the risks, his widow Lauren recalled. The flight was originally scheduled for Friday but was delayed.

### Ebola: "Running After the Disease"

The Ebola outbreak in East Africa has produced more than 800 confirmed cases and nearly 200 deaths. Jean Kaseya, director general of the Africa CDC, warned it "could become the worst on record" if transmission rates do not flatten. Health workers face steep obstacles: distrust of authorities, violence in eastern DRC. "We are running after the disease," said Bruno Michon of the Red Cross.

### The Fed's Stalemate

The Federal Reserve held interest rates at 3.5% to 3.75% for a fourth straight meeting — Kevin Warsh's first as chair. The vote was unanimous, but the unanimity "belies the very challenging position" Warsh faces. He was handpicked by Trump, who wants rate cuts, but the Iran war has unleashed inflation that makes cuts impossible. Trump's response: "It's all right, whatever."

### Oil at 1983 Lows

U.S. government oil stockpiles are poised to hit their lowest levels since 1983. The war in Iran has drained strategic reserves that companies and governments "stash in giant steel storage tanks and underground salt caverns." Peace in the Strait of Hormuz is necessary but not sufficient: supply chains will take months to normalize.

### The Fertilizer Squeeze

Mosaic Company lost $58 million in the quarter ending March 30 because sulfur — a fifth of the global supply transits the Strait of Hormuz — has become prohibitively expensive. "If we're losing money every ton, the total losses can mount quickly," said Ben Pratt. Farmers are cutting back. Recovery is a "2028 story."

### Death Penalty Consideration

Federal prosecutors told a judge they are deciding whether to seek the death penalty against Rahmanullah Lakanwal, charged with killing a National Guard specialist. D.C. abolished the death penalty in 1981, so federal charges are the only path to capital punishment. The Justice Department has begun the review process.

Jeromy Smith / Lauren Smith — the engineer and his widow, narrating the human cost of aging infrastructure. Ben Pratt — Mosaic's VP, describing a supply chain crisis from half a world away. Kevin Warsh — the Fed chair trapped between his president and economic reality. Jean Kaseya — the Africa CDC chief warning of a historic outbreak. Bruno Michon — the Red Cross responder "running after the disease." Rahmanullah Lakanwal — the defendant facing potential federal execution.

### Analytical Depth

Laozi's Hardness: The B-52 has been in service since 1955 — hard, durable, reliable. But its hardness (age, rigid structure, obsolete systems) is what killed its crew. "What is rigid and hard is close to death." The softness of newer technologies was being added, but the old frame failed first.

Friction cannot be eliminated; it can only be absorbed by reserve capacity. Oil reserves, FEMA staffing, and alliance trust — the shock absorbers — are all depleted.

A quick, decisive intervention was not achieved; the question is whether political attention can be sustained through a year-long struggle.

### Finland Lifts Nuclear Weapons Ban (Article 10)

Finnish lawmakers voted to overturn a Cold War-era total ban on nuclear weapons, allowing allied weapons to be imported, transported through, or held on Finnish territory. Finland shares an 830-mile border with Russia and joined NATO in 2023. "The nuclear deterrence is, in the end, the guarantor of peace in Europe," said Heikki Autto, chair of the defense committee. The move is defensive and pragmatic — a pre-emptive strengthening of position before a threat materializes fully.

Named Human: Heikki Autto — the Finnish parliamentarian articulating the logic of deterrence as peace.
