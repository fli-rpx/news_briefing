Geopolitics & Conflict

The Strait of Hormuz: Iran's Asymmetric Gambit

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps struck a container ship passing through the Strait of Hormuz on Thursday, firing a drone that hit the vessel and brought maritime traffic through the world's most critical energy chokepoint to a halt. The attack, confirmed by both U.S. and Iranian officials, came just hours after Tehran warned ships that the only permissible route through the 33-kilometer-wide waterway lay within Iranian waters — an implicit rejection of the alternative passage hugging the Omani coast that many vessels had adopted in recent months after earlier disruptions. The strike contradicted President Trump's public assurances that Iran did not control the strait and that it had reopened to normal shipping. Oil markets reacted instantly: Brent crude rose more than 2 percent, to roughly $75 a barrel, while West Texas Intermediate climbed a similar margin, to around $72 a barrel. The International Maritime Organization suspended its plan to evacuate seafarers from hundreds of ships left stranded in the Persian Gulf.

The timing was particularly treacherous. The attack followed the announcement of a preliminary truce in the U.S.-Iran war, and it threw the durability of that ceasefire into deep doubt. Secretary of State Marco Rubio was in Bahrain on Thursday meeting with Gulf Arab leaders, trying to allay security concerns that the Strait crisis had reignited. The diplomatic architecture around the truce was always fragile — and now it has been pierced. The Operations analysis, drawing on Han Fei's concept of shi (positional advantage), rightly identifies the Strait as a textbook case of asymmetric power: Iran does not need to match U.S. naval capacity to control one of the world's great bottlenecks. It only needs to prove, repeatedly, that it can interdict, and the uncertainty alone does the work of a blockade. The Strategy analysis, through Clausewitz, identifies the Strait as the secondary center of gravity in the global energy system — the physical point where the mass of 20 percent of the world's oil transit concentrates. The U.S. has not neutralized it.

Calibrated view: 30% probability the ceasefire holds through the NATO summit in July. Because the strike came after the truce was declared, both sides have demonstrated that the form of peace (the announced truce) and the substance of conflict (the strike) can coexist, a pattern the Philosophy analysis, through Mao's dialectics, identifies as a "paper truce" — the shell of resolution concealing continued struggle.

The NATO Tightrope

Three days before the Strait strike, NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte sat in the Oval Office and performed what may be the most consequential diplomatic intervention of the year. President Trump was mid-tirade about European allies, complaining about their slow defense spending and their refusal to support U.S. military operations against Iran, when Rutte — a former Dutch prime minister who has earned a reputation as the European leader most skilled at managing Trump — interrupted him. He set up posters around the room. One chart showed the "Trump Trillion," the surge in European and Canadian defense spending since Trump first took office. Another homed in on the past two years, when NATO spending had exceeded $250 billion. "This is your evidence," Rutte told the president, framing the entire increase as Trump's personal achievement — an approach that visibly defused the president's anger.

The Connector analysis identifies this meeting as the single instance of successful "ventral vagal" co-regulation in the entire news cycle — a rare moment when a dysregulated nervous system (Trump's anger) was regulated back toward social engagement through skilled, non-threatening connection. The deeper strategic insight, however, is that Rutte's de-escalation did not resolve the underlying friction. European allies continue to see the Iran war as "ill-considered and illegal," as one diplomat put it, and the goal of the July 7 NATO summit in Ankara, Turkey, has been reduced to the modest ambition of avoiding an explosion. The Operations analysis, through Peter Drucker's lens of executive effectiveness, identifies Rutte as the only actor across all 15 articles who operates competently across every dimension: he focuses on contribution (keeping the alliance intact), makes Trump's strengths productive (his vanity about driving spending), prepares alternatives, and uses disagreement productively ("I disagree slightly"). The Senate Surrender

The Iran crisis also produced one of the most striking displays of institutional submission in recent memory. On Wednesday, Trump confronted Senate Republicans at a closed-door lunch, excoriating them for allowing the resolution to pass. By Wednesday night, Republican leaders had brought a nearly identical measure to the floor — and defeated it, 50 to 47, with one senator voting "present." Two senators who had voted for the resolution on Tuesday shifted: Bill Cassidy of Louisiana, who said he changed his vote after a White House briefing from Vice President JD Vance and envoy Steve Witkoff; and Rand Paul of Kentucky, who voted "present" and acknowledged that Trump's anger had influenced his decision.

Venezuela: The Alliance Test

Back-to-back earthquakes — a 7.2-magnitude tremor followed 39 seconds later by a 7.5 — have devastated Venezuela, killing at least 235 people and injuring more than 4,300. The doublet, one of the most powerful tectonic events to strike the country in a century, leveled entire buildings in the port city of La Guaira, where residents dug through rubble with their bare hands because no rescue workers arrived. The disaster is now testing the "forced alliance" between the United States and Venezuela — the arrangement that followed Trump's military operation to remove Nicolás Maduro, which redirected Venezuelan oil and gold to the United States. Rubio said rescue teams from Virginia and California would assist, and the Defense Department would help deliver aid. But he did not say how much money the U.S. would allocate, and the disbanding of USAID last year has hollowed out the institutional capacity to mount a large-scale humanitarian response.

U.S. Politics & The Trump Trifecta

The Supreme Court's Deference Cascade

Thursday was the most consequential day of the Supreme Court's current term, producing three rulings that collectively expanded conservative power across immigration, corporate liability, and gun rights. The most far-reaching was the immigration decision: a 6-to-3 ruling, divided along ideological lines, that allowed the Trump administration to terminate Temporary Protected Status for 350,000 Haitians and 6,100 Syrians, clearing a path for their potential deportation. The program, created by Congress with bipartisan support in 1990, had provided legal status to people from countries deemed unsafe due to war, natural disaster, or crisis. The United States has a long history of intervention in both Haiti and Syria — the 2010 earthquake that killed 220,000 Haitians prompted the original TPS extension; Syria's TPS was granted after civil war erupted in 2011. Now, with both countries still mired in violence and instability, the protections are being withdrawn.

The Philosophy analysis, through Mao's dialectics, reads this as the old legal superstructure (Congress's 1990 bipartisan framework) being broken open by a new concentration of executive and judicial power. The contradiction between the theory of "temporary" protection and the reality of permanent displacement has forced a resolution — and the Court chose executive discretion over legislative intent. The Connector analysis notes the historical guilt cycling beneath the surface: the U.S. intervened in Haiti and Syria, and now it abandons the populations those interventions helped displace.

The $250 Question

Trump's reimagining of American identity continued with the revelation that the Treasury Department is working on design concepts for a $250 bill bearing his portrait — the biggest overhaul to U.S. currency in more than a century. The proposal faces a legal hurdle: a federal law forbids any living person from appearing on paper currency. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said last month he did not find "anything untoward" about having the current president on the 250th anniversary bill. The initiative is a fusion of Trump's personal brand with the nation's identity, and it has already drawn comparisons to the 2016 movement to replace Andrew Jackson with Harriet Tubman on the $20 bill — a proposal that stirred contentious national debate. As Shailendra Bhandare, an expert on the history of money at the University of Oxford, observed: "Currency designs can be controversial because they are imbued with a sense of national identity, therefore they are consumed with immense passion. In today's polarized world, this has the potential to become an instant fault line."

The Abortion Prosecution Threshold

On the fourth anniversary of the Supreme Court's decision to overturn Roe v. Wade, a group of more than 60 conservative influencers, antiabortion leaders, and pastors signed a petition calling for the removal of "legal immunities" that have protected women who get abortions from prosecution. The petition represents a significant shift within the antiabortion movement, which has been largely unified around the position that doctors — not women — should face punishment. But the movement is frustrated: more abortions are happening now than when Roe fell, driven largely by the growing availability of abortion pills that have made bans effectively unenforceable. The Texas Republican Party's state convention earlier this month endorsed repealing legal protections for women by a thunderous voice vote. And the largest antiabortion group in Texas is developing a strategy to target a narrower slice of women — those with medical licenses — by threatening to revoke their credentials if they are caught taking abortion pills.

The Philosophy analysis, through Nietzsche's concept of the "ascetic ideal," identifies this as the will to power turned against life itself: the movement, frustrated that prohibition has failed, doubles down, creating ever-stricter laws and willing suffering — both its own (frustration, political losses) and others' (prosecution, imprisonment). The Philosophy analysis, through Mao, sees this as a textbook case of theory being negated by practice: the original theory (punish providers, not women) has failed to produce the desired result (fewer abortions), so the theory must change. Whether the new theory can succeed is another question entirely — prosecuting women may trigger a political backlash that reverses the movement's gains.

Economy & Markets

Inflation's Stubborn Grip

The Commerce Department reported Thursday that the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge, the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index, accelerated again in May. Overall prices jumped 0.4 percent during the month, reaching 4.1 percent on an annual basis. Core inflation — which strips out food and energy — notched 3.4 percent, a fresh high since 2023. The data arrived at a moment of deep uncertainty. A preliminary truce in the Iran war had raised hopes that the worst of the inflation surge might be over — oil prices have dropped since the truce announcement, gasoline costs are beginning to fall, and airfares and shipping fees are poised to follow. But the underlying trend was already troubling before the war began, and the conflict has only worsened it.

Alan Detmeister, a former Fed economist now at UBS, offered a sobering forecast: even if all the positive scenarios pan out — the Iran truce holds, tariff effects fade, housing inflation decelerates, and AI-driven productivity gains materialize — it will still take two more years for the Fed to reach its 2 percent target, after having overshot it for half a decade. The risks, he added, are all pointing up. The new Fed chairman, Kevin M. Warsh, has vowed to deliver price stability but has refused to offer explicit signals about the path forward for policy — a stance that the Operations analysis, through Drucker, identifies as organizational paralysis. The Fed cannot agree on what the problem is (how patient can they be?), cannot agree on how to measure it (what is the best gauge of underlying pressure?), and cannot communicate a clear decision. The central bank's inability to reach an effective decision on its core function is a failure of contribution.

The Roundup Reckoning

In a separate but economically consequential ruling, the Supreme Court sided 7-to-2 with Bayer, the manufacturer of the Roundup weedkiller, overturning a $1.25 million jury award for John Durnell, a Missouri gardener who claimed that decades of exposure to the product caused his non-Hodgkin lymphoma. The majority, in an opinion by Justice Kavanaugh, held that federal pesticide law pre-empted Durnell's state-law failure-to-warn claim, because a cancer warning on Roundup's label would conflict with the label approved by the EPA. The decision is likely to determine the fate of thousands of similar lawsuits pending against Bayer across state and federal courts.

The ruling featured an unusual cross-ideological alliance: Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, a liberal, was joined in dissent by Justice Neil Gorsuch, a conservative. Justice Jackson wrote that the majority had left Durnell "without a remedy for the significant harms he has suffered." The Connector analysis identifies this Jackson-Gorsuch alliance as a rare micro-instance of principle bridging ideology — a fleeting ventral vagal connection in an otherwise polarized institution. The Operations analysis, through Drucker, raises a harder question: is the decision effective or merely correct? If Bayer faces global regulatory backlash and reputational damage regardless of the legal outcome, the Supreme Court's ruling was legally sound but operationally hollow.

Europe's Heat Wave: The Second Blow

Europe is in the grip of its second heat wave since May, and the human toll is mounting. In Spain, where temperatures exceeded 38 degrees Celsius (100 Fahrenheit) over several days, statistical models from the Health Institute of Carlos III suggest more than 200 deaths may ultimately be attributable to the heat. In Italy, five people have died from heat exposure this week, including outdoor workers and a homeless man in Naples. In France, at least 40 people have drowned since the heat wave began, many of them teenagers swimming in unsupervised areas, and prosecutors have opened investigations into the deaths of three children found in parked cars.

The heat wave is a structural contradiction — the second blow in a summer that is testing Europe's capacity to adapt to extreme weather. The Right to Carry

The Supreme Court's third major ruling of the day struck down a Hawaii law that required gun owners to obtain permission before carrying concealed firearms onto private property open to the public — grocery stores, coffee shops, gas stations, and other spaces where daily life unfolds. The 6-to-3 decision, split along ideological lines, held that Hawaii's restriction violated the Second Amendment. Writing for the majority, Justice Alito argued that the law "hobbles what the Second Amendment protects: the right of Americans to carry arms for self-defense as they go about their daily lives." In dissent, Justice Jackson — joined by Justice Sotomayor — wrote that states could protect private property rights "by requiring those wishing to carry guns onto private property to get express consent from the property owner before doing so," and accused the majority of "protecting guns, not consistently preserving any principle of law."

The Connector analysis identifies a subtle but significant social consequence: the decision may undermine the implicit sense of safety in shared public spaces. When anyone can carry concealed weapons into a grocery store or coffee shop, the social contract of safety — the ventral vagal trust that allows strangers to share space without hypervigilance — is disrupted. The Philosophy analysis, through Nietzsche, sees Justice Jackson's dissent as a moment of unmasking: she sees through the legal reasoning to the will beneath, recognizing that the majority's objective is protecting guns, not preserving any consistent principle. The Strategy analysis places this ruling in the context of a Supreme Court that is the institutional center of gravity for conservative power — one that will outlast Trump and continue to shape American life long after he leaves office.

The Heavy Hand in Colombia

Among the fifteen articles in this cycle, the deepest investigative story — the one with the most significant implications for democratic norms, international law, and the character of American power — is the account of Trump's intervention in Colombia's presidential election. This is not a story about a tweet or a phone call. It is the story of a coordinated, multi-actor operation: the president's endorsement, the engagement of Republican members of Congress to mobilize diaspora voters, and the detention — on U.S. soil — of a Colombian activist who had urged Colombians in the United States not to vote for the Trump-endorsed candidate.

Trump's influence reached deep into Colombia's presidential election, where right-wing candidate Abelardo De La Espriella won by a razor-thin margin on Sunday. The victory was built on a three-part intervention: Trump's "complete and total endorsement" of De La Espriella; support from Republican members of Congress who helped mobilize conservative Colombians abroad; and the detention, just days before the election, of an activist critical of De La Espriella who had urged Colombians in the United States not to vote for him. The activist was detained under a memo signed by Secretary Rubio, asserting that the individual had been "interfering with U.S. foreign policy." Experts called the coordination unprecedented in the 21st century. "The attempt to quash dissent in a coordinated manner with the Trump administration, extending from Colombia to U.S. soil — I can't think of anything in recent times that resembles that," said Alexander Main of the Center for Economic and Policy Research.

The detention was authorized by a memo from Secretary of State Marco Rubio, who asserted that the activist had been interfering with U.S. foreign policy. This is the most legally and normatively significant detail in the entire news cycle. A foreign national, present in the United States, was detained by U.S. immigration authorities for expressing political opinions about a foreign election — an election in which the U.S. president had explicitly taken sides. The authority invoked was not a criminal statute but the secretary of state's foreign policy powers, a legal theory that, if stretched to its logical conclusion, could authorize the detention of any foreign national whose political speech displeases the administration.

The razor-thin margin of victory — won in part through votes cast outside the country, mobilized by Republican lawmakers — means that De La Espriella's mandate is inherently contested. The activist's detention will be a rallying point for opposition. The U.S. has entangled itself not with a stable ally but with a leader whose electoral legitimacy will be questioned from the day he takes office. 百年变局下的战略僵持：美国的"纸老虎"困境

你看现在美国什么局面？伊朗在霍尔木兹海峡打了一架无人机，把全球油价打上去了；特朗普跟国会的拉锯战，把自家党派的住房法案给"拉"没了；北约盟友怕的不是俄罗斯，怕的是自己盟主掀桌子。用咱们老百姓的话说，这就好比——第一，你家里请了个装修队，队长天天跟工头吵架，活没干完，工具先砸了。这叫"制度内耗"，美国人管它叫"trifecta"（三连胜），我看是"trifail"（三连败）。

再说这个货币的事。特朗普要在250美元钞票上印自己的头像。你想想——第二，这就跟小区楼下理发店，Tony老师非要把自己照片贴在招牌上，还说要取代原来那个造型师。活人上钞票，法律明文禁止的，他偏要干。这说明什么？说明美国的"虚体"（制度）已经被"实体"（个人野心）绑架了。

第三，北约那个事更有意思。秘书长吕特去白宫，拿一堆图表摆出来，跟哄小孩似的说："总统先生，这是您的功劳！"——特朗普立马消气了。这跟什么呢？跟胡同口大爷哄孙子一模一样："乖，这糖是你自己挣的。"堂堂全球最强大军事联盟，要靠哄才能运转——这不是战略疲劳是什么？

我经常说，美国现在处于一个战略僵持期：它力量还在，但使不出来，像一个人想打架，腿却抽筋了。伊朗知道这一点，欧洲也知道，拉美也知道。这就是"百年未有之大变局"的核心——不是美国变弱了，而是它变混乱了。混乱比弱更可怕，因为弱可以养，混乱是内伤。

这个局怎么破？我的判断是：美国需要一次真正的战略收缩，但特朗普政府既没有这个意愿，也没有这个能力。所以我们还要继续看这场"大戏"——精彩着呢，别走开。

——金灿荣，2026年6月

2026年6月26日 — 美元的退潮与康波冬天的降临

又一轮美元潮汐正在退去。美联储核心PCE飙至4.1%，新的掌门人沃什却拒绝给出任何明确信号。这不是技术性犹豫，这是中央银行的系统性瘫痪——当印钞机无法再转，水龙头关不上也拧不开，全球体系就进入了康德拉季耶夫冬季最危险的阶段。

看看外围发生了什么。伊朗在霍尔木兹海峡打了一架无人机，布伦特原油立刻弹到75美元。这不是偶发冲突，这是美元信用收缩时，资源国的必然反抗。外围世界正在崩解——委内瑞拉遭遇7.2和7.5双震，235人死亡，搜救队却到不了现场。为什么？因为国家能力已经被债务周期掏空了。拉瓜伊拉居民徒手扒开碎石寻找亲人，而美国嘴上说支援，USAID早就被裁掉了。套用我常说的一句话：债务周期的高潮退去，裸泳的人不是不想上岸，是根本没有岸了。

美国的"三权全控"式府会，却连一个住房法案都推不动。特朗普把自己的头像印到250美元纸币上——这是美元信用最后的狂欢。当"活人不能上钞票"的法律都可以被绕开，你就能看出，帝国的货币体系已经从经济工具退化成个人崇拜的装饰品。这就是典型的"货币战争"第三章：当霸主开始消耗货币信用而非积累时，长波周期的下行期就不可逆转了。

现在的问题不是美联储会不会降息，而是它即便降息也救不了谁。康波冬天的本质是结构性萧条，不是流动性短缺。外围已经塌了，核心正在空转。而那个最清醒的人，是一个拿着图表去安抚暴怒总统的荷兰人。这本身就说明了一切。

——宋鸿兵
