NATO's Existential Summit — Can Europe Lead?

The headlines from Ankara are diplomatic boilerplate, but the subtext is historic. NATO leaders gathering in Turkey this week face a question that would have been unthinkable five years ago: can the alliance function without the United States as its senior partner? President Trump's strategy is transparent — pressure European members to carry the burden, then claim credit for their increased spending. 23 of 32 members now meet the 2% of GDP target (up from single digits in 2015). But spending levels mask capability gaps. Even at 2%, European militaries cannot replace U.S. intelligence, airlift, command-and-control, or nuclear deterrence. The real question is whether Europe has the political will to act as a unified strategic power. Prediction: The Ankara summit will produce the weakest final statement in the alliance's history — a deliberately vague compromise that satisfies no one. (85%)

Russia Returns to the Olympic Fold

The Solow Paradox 2.0 — AI Everywhere, Measured Nowhere

Ben Casselman's analysis reveals a growing disconnect: corporations are investing $200B+ annually in AI, yet productivity growth remains stuck at ~1.2%. This mirrors the 1987 Solow Computer Paradox ("computers are everywhere except in the productivity statistics"). Three explanations compete: (1) a lag effect — AI's impact takes years to show up in aggregate data; (2) mismeasurement — GDP and productivity statistics were designed for industrial economies and miss AI's quality improvements and free services; (3) concentration — AI's benefits are real but captured by a small number of firms. Policy implications: if we cannot measure AI's impact, we cannot effectively tax, regulate, or subsidize it. Prediction: BLS will announce a major revision to productivity measurement in the tech sector within 12 months. (70%)

Regulation Under Uncertainty

The SCOTUS agency ruling creates a new risk premium for long-term business investment.
